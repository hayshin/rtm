\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Information Extraction from Unstructured Clinical Data}
While Electronic Health Records (EHRs) store vast quantities of data, a large portion of actionable clinical information—such as discharge summaries, pathology reports, and imaging texts—remains trapped in unstructured formats \cite{herland2020beyond}. Because manual chart review is a highly laborious and expensive process, there has been a significant push toward developing Natural Language Processing (NLP) tools and hybrid information extraction platforms to automate the retrieval of these quality indicators \cite{wang2018extracting}. Furthermore, bridging the semantic gap between unstructured narratives (such as unstructured patient feedback or observational notes) and structured health-care data is critical; applying semantics and affective information processing can effectively map unstructured text to structured clinical quality metrics \cite{cambria2015bridging}.

\subsection{Combining Data Modalities for Clinical Phenotyping}
Automatic clinical phenotyping heavily benefits from the integration of both structured and unstructured data \cite{hong2021phenotyping}. Recent empirical studies utilizing deep learning architectures, such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, demonstrate that combining structured measurements (e.g., vital signs, laboratory results) with vector representations of unstructured clinical notes yields statistically significant improvements in predicting complex clinical phenotypes \cite{hong2021phenotyping}. Relying on unstructured text alone or structured data alone often falls short, as text contains crucial diagnostic context missing from tabular data, making their combination the most robust approach for identifying disease patterns \cite{hong2021phenotyping}.

\subsection{Advances in Speech Technology and Medical Transcription}
A major source of unstructured healthcare data originates from spoken patient-provider interactions and physician dictations, which present unique transcription challenges due to complex medical jargon, varying accents, and ambient hospital noise \cite{schuller2020speech}. Recent advancements in automatic speech recognition (ASR) via large-scale weak supervision have produced highly robust models, such as OpenAI's Whisper, which was trained on 680,000 hours of audio data \cite{radford2022whisper}. Whisper has demonstrated the ability to approach human-level transcription accuracy in a zero-shot setting without requiring dataset-specific fine-tuning \cite{radford2022whisper}. Beyond standard transcription, Whisper has also been successfully validated for automating psycholinguistic oral proficiency assessments, achieving near-perfect alignment with human raters \cite{wagner2025assessing}.

To further address the intricacies of clinical speech, researchers have begun integrating Large Language Models (LLMs) to post-process and refine ASR outputs \cite{singh2024sound}. By employing techniques like Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, LLMs can effectively correct phonetic misinterpretations and significantly reduce the Medical Concept Word Error Rate (MC-WER), thereby preserving the semantic integrity of critical clinical terms \cite{singh2024sound}. The rigorous evaluation of these enhanced ASR systems requires representative datasets; recent initiatives have synthesized large-scale medical dialogue corpora augmented with controlled hospital background noise to accurately benchmark ASR robustness in clinical environments \cite{oliveira2024medical}.

\subsection{Interoperability and the FHIR Standard}
As automated systems successfully process unstructured data into actionable insights, exchanging this information across diverse health information systems becomes paramount \cite{herland2020beyond}. The Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) standard has emerged as an agile, RESTful API approach to solve global clinical data exchange challenges \cite{bender2021fhir}. By utilizing modular "resources" (e.g., patient, condition, observation), FHIR enables granular access to electronic health records and supports modern interoperability frameworks like SMART on FHIR \cite{bender2021fhir}. This allows for the standardized integration of research tools, mobile applications, and clinical decision support systems, successfully addressing many of the integration barriers inherent in legacy HL7 architecture \cite{bender2021fhir}.
